About Me

From soldering irons to solar cells

I got into engineering the unglamorous way: by taking things apart and not always managing to put them back together. What stuck wasn't any single gadget — it was the moment a circuit I'd built actually did what I designed it to do. That feeling is the reason I'm at Georgia Tech studying Electrical and Computer Engineering, and it's the same feeling I chase now in coursework, in lab, and in side projects.

My focus areas are embedded systems, aerospace, and clean energy — three fields that turn out to share a lot of the same underlying problems: how do you get a system to sense its environment, make a decision, and act on limited power? That question is what drew me to Georgia Tech's VIP (Vertically Integrated Projects) program, where I work with the M.A.R.S. team on a NASA Kennedy Space Center / JPL research partnership investigating piezoelectric and triboelectric energy harvesting — essentially, pulling usable electrical energy out of vibration and motion that would otherwise go to waste. It's early-stage, unglamorous research some days and genuinely exciting on others, and it's given me a real look at how energy-systems engineering actually gets done.

"The circuits get more complicated. The question stays the same: where does the energy come from, and where does it go?"

Outside of research, I'm building out the fundamentals — working through RC/RL transient analysis, Thévenin and Norton equivalents, and op-amp design in my circuits coursework, and applying computer architecture concepts (RISC-V assembly, memory layout) from my systems classes to embedded projects. I'm also active in the National Society of Black Engineers (NSBE) and the Society of Women Engineers (SWE) at Georgia Tech, communities that have shaped how I think about engineering as a collaborative, not solitary, discipline.

I speak English and Mandarin Chinese fluently and am working on Spanish. I like that engineering, like language, rewards patience with unfamiliar syntax — whether that syntax is a foreign grammar rule or a stubborn compiler error.

Career Goals

The roadmap: here to there

My long-term goal is to work as a systems or power engineer at an organization operating at the edge of aerospace or clean-energy technology — NASA, SpaceX, or a renewable energy company solving hard power-delivery problems. My Professional Development Plan at Georgia Tech is built around the Devices and Energy Systems thread, which lines up directly with that goal: it's the track that goes deepest on power electronics, energy conversion, and the physical devices that make energy systems work.

Between now and graduation, the plan has four stages. I'm currently in the first: building the fundamentals through core ECE coursework (circuits, electromagnetics, computer architecture) while getting hands-on research exposure through VIP M.A.R.S. The next stage is proving I can apply that knowledge outside the classroom — I'm targeting NASA's L'SPACE pre-professional program, specifically the Mission Concept Academy and the Proposal Writing and Evaluation Experience, as a structured way to get real exposure to how NASA mission proposals are built and reviewed. From there, I want to pursue an aerospace- or energy-focused internship or co-op to get direct industry experience, and finally to graduate positioned for a full-time role in power systems, embedded avionics, or energy-harvesting hardware.

Discovery Project: Python Logic Circuit Simulator

In Progress
Python Digital Logic Circuit Simulation

For my ECE 1100 Individual Discovery Project, I'm building a Python-based logic circuit simulator: a program that lets a user construct digital logic circuits out of basic gates (AND, OR, NOT, XOR, and their derivatives) and simulate their behavior without needing physical hardware or a paid simulation license. The idea came out of a frustration I noticed in my own coursework — most of the free tools for visualizing digital logic are either too simplistic to be useful for real circuit design or too complex to be approachable for someone just learning the material.

Getting to this final concept took a few iterations. I initially considered projects centered on embedded sensor systems and small energy-harvesting demos, drawing on what I'd been exposed to through VIP M.A.R.S., but scoped those down after realizing the hardware procurement and testing timeline didn't fit a single-semester Discovery Project. The logic circuit simulator won out because it let me combine two things I actually wanted to get better at — software architecture in Python and the digital logic fundamentals from my coursework — into a tool I could build, test, and iterate on entirely in code.

The simulator represents each circuit as a directed graph, where logic gates are nodes and wires are edges carrying boolean signal values. A propagation engine evaluates the graph in topological order so that multi-gate circuits resolve correctly regardless of how they're wired, and a simple text- or grid-based interface lets a user place gates, connect them, and step through the simulation to see signal values change in real time. Planned features include truth-table auto-generation for any constructed circuit, saving and loading circuit designs, and a basic library of common building blocks (half-adders, multiplexers, flip-flops) so users can build on smaller circuits instead of starting from individual gates every time.

This project won't be complete by the ePortfolio publish deadline — per the assignment structure, it's expected to be finished in time for the Discovery Project Showcase later in the semester. I'll be updating this section with build progress, screenshots of the working interface, and a link to the source repository as development continues.

  • Graph-based circuit representation with topological signal propagation
  • Support for AND / OR / NOT / XOR / NAND / NOR gates
  • Planned: automatic truth-table generation and save/load for circuit designs
  • Planned: reusable component library (adders, muxes, flip-flops)

Buzz Blitz — ESP32 Handheld Game

Complete
C / PlatformIO ESP32 Embedded Systems

Built for ECE 2035 (Computer Organization), Buzz Blitz is a side-scrolling handheld game written in C and deployed to an ESP32 microcontroller using PlatformIO. I implemented the core map infrastructure and collision system from scratch, then layered on gameplay mechanics including gravity, a "stinger beam" attack with splash damage, and a full replay and leaderboard system so players could track and revisit past runs. The project also included pause/resume state handling — a small feature that turned out to require a surprising amount of care to get right on constrained embedded memory.

This was my first real experience with the full embedded development loop: writing C close to the hardware, managing limited memory and processing budget on a microcontroller, and debugging behavior that only showed up on-device. It directly informs how I approach the sensor and power-constrained systems I now work with in VIP research.
